System Architecture & Design Choices

Executive Summary: The system implements a reactive ARQ (Automatic Repeat reQuest) protocol using non-blocking UDP sockets in C++, optimized for low-latency recovery over a hostile, burst-loss network.

1. The Sender Architecture

The sender acts as a fast-forwarding cache. As frames arrive from the harness source, the sender immediately forwards them to the hostile relay while simultaneously storing a copy in a std::map (indexed by the sequence number). It continuously polls a dedicated feedback port for Negative Acknowledgements (NACKs). If a 4-byte NACK arrives, the sender performs a constant-time lookup in the map and immediately retransmits the requested frame.

2. The Receiver Architecture (Jitter Buffer)

The receiver's primary role is to reorder jittered packets and proactively identify data loss. It utilizes a std::map as a dynamically sorting Jitter Buffer. As packets arrive, they are inserted into the map. The receiver maintains a pointer to the expected_seq. If the buffer contains the expected sequence, it is flushed to the player port and the pointer increments.

3. Design Choice: ARQ vs. FEC

Given the constraints of a 2.0x bandwidth cap and a strict deadline miss rate of ≤ 1%, the architecture leverages ARQ over Forward Error Correction (FEC).

  • Overhead Efficiency: Sending proactive FEC data scales bandwidth usage linearly. By using 4-byte NACKs, the feedback overhead is negligible (yielding a total bandwidth overhead of ~1.3x).
  • Burst-Loss Resilience (Throttling): To combat severe burst losses without exceeding the bandwidth cap, the receiver scans for sequence gaps and throttles its NACK requests to exactly once every 25 milliseconds. This prevents NACK flooding during heavy drops while ensuring the sender has enough time to recover the missing frames before the 250ms playout deadline.
